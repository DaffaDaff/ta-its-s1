\begin{abstract}
  Sea turtle populations continue to decline, and conservation programmes need to
  identify individuals without the cost and invasiveness of satellite tagging. We
  present a re-identification (Re-ID) system for sea turtle head images built on a
  Vision Transformer (ViT) backbone pretrained with DINOv3, trained under a strict
  open-set identity split on the SeaTurtleIDHeads dataset. A baseline configuration
  derived from literature conventions reaches 78.3\% Rank-1 and 84.9\% mAP; a tuned
  configuration reaches 90.4\% Rank-1 and 93.5\% mAP. A one-factor-at-a-time study
  over 19 parameters shows that the backbone learning rate alone accounts for
  roughly 78\% of the total improvement, and that individual gains are strongly
  sub-additive: summing them predicts an arithmetically impossible 112\% Rank-1,
  while only 36\% of the predicted gain is realised when combined. Applied to
  SeaTurtleID2022 without any retraining, the model retains 81.1\% Rank-1 and
  97.2\% Rank-5, confirming that the learned embedding transfers across capture
  domains. The system's real limitation appears only when unregistered individuals
  must be rejected: Registered Rank-1 falls to 55.3\% with a global threshold and
  56.7\% with a background class, both at a false-reject rate near 42\%. Two
  fundamentally different decision rules converge on nearly the same operating
  point because top-1 cosine scores of registered and unregistered queries are
  confined to a narrow band (ROC-AUC $0.77$--$0.79$). In the worst failures the
  correct identity still ranks first and is discarded by the rejection stage. The
  bottleneck is therefore score calibration, not representation quality.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Sea turtles are among the most ecologically significant yet threatened groups of
marine reptiles. Indonesia alone hosts six of the world's seven species~\cite{ario2016}.
Populations have declined sharply due to coastal development that destroys
nesting habitats and to illegal egg collection and poaching~\cite{ario2016}.
Documented cases in West Sulawesi between 2016 and 2019 reported more than twenty
turtles killed for their eggs and meat~\cite{nur2022}.

Effective conservation requires tracking individuals non-invasively over long
periods in order to estimate population size, study migration, and monitor
reproduction. The conventional approach, satellite tagging, attaches a GPS
transmitter to the animal. It is accurate but expensive, vulnerable to device
malfunction, and imposes stress during attachment~\cite{hays2018}.

Computer-vision re-identification (Re-ID) is a non-invasive alternative. Re-ID
recognises a previously observed individual from new images using unique visual
characteristics~\cite{zheng2016}. For sea turtles the scale (scute) patterns on
the head remain stable throughout an individual's lifetime~\cite{carpentier2016},
functioning as a natural biometric. Vision Transformers~\cite{dosovitskiy2021}
combined with self-supervised pretraining such as DINOv3~\cite{simeoni2025}
produce rich, transferable representations well suited to this fine-grained task.

Most wildlife Re-ID work reports closed-gallery retrieval accuracy. Field
deployment, however, is inherently open-set in a second and harder sense: a
photograph may show an animal that has never been registered, and the system must
say so. We find that this distinction dominates practical performance. Our
contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A DINOv3 ViT Re-ID pipeline for sea turtle head images, trained under an
        open-set identity split, reaching 90.4\% Rank-1 and 93.5\% mAP on held-out
        identities.
  \item A sensitivity map over 19 parameters showing that the backbone learning
        rate is the single dominant factor, and that per-parameter gains measured by
        one-factor-at-a-time (OFAT) tuning are sub-additive and cannot be summed.
  \item A cross-dataset evaluation on SeaTurtleID2022 without retraining,
        quantifying the cost of domain change at 9.3 percentage points (pp) of Rank-1.
  \item Evidence that under unregistered-identity rejection the limiting factor is
        score calibration rather than embedding quality: two structurally different
        rejection rules reach nearly identical operating points, and correct matches
        remain rank-1 even among the most confident errors.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
% -------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Sea Turtle Individual Identification}

Early automated systems used neural network ensembles on facial scute images of
green turtles~\cite{carter2014}. Carpentier et al.~\cite{carpentier2016} validated
the long-term stability of facial scute patterns over 11 years, confirming their
suitability as persistent biometric markers. Alternative body regions such as
front-flipper scales have also been explored~\cite{gatto2018,dunbar2023}, with
some showing higher accuracy under specific conditions.

Adam et al.~\cite{adam2023} introduced SeaTurtleID2022, a 13-year longitudinal
dataset of 438 loggerhead turtles, together with a time-aware open-set protocol
and a baseline integrating Hybrid Task Cascade segmentation with ArcFace feature
extraction, reaching 86.8\% Rank-1. Nguyen et al.~\cite{nguyen2023} evaluated
AlexNet, GoogLeNet, VGG-19, and ResNet-50 on facial scute images, with ResNet-50
reaching 95.3\% accuracy on a mixed dataset under a closed-set protocol.
\v{C}erm\'ak et al.~\cite{cermak2024} introduced WildlifeDatasets and
MegaDescriptor, the first multi-species Re-ID foundation model, outperforming
general pretrained models including CLIP and DINOv2. Papafitsoros et
al.~\cite{papafitsoros2025} improved accuracy further by exploiting left-right
facial profile similarities within the same individual.

\subsection{Vision Transformers and Metric Learning}

ViT~\cite{dosovitskiy2021} applies the Transformer architecture directly to
sequences of image patches; self-attention captures global spatial relationships
in a single layer, unlike the bounded receptive field of convolutions.
DINO~\cite{caron2021}, DINOv2~\cite{oquab2024}, and DINOv3~\cite{simeoni2025}
provide self-supervised ViT features that transfer strongly to downstream
recognition without labels. Re-ID itself is usually framed as metric
learning~\cite{schroff2015}, with ArcFace~\cite{deng2019} enforcing an additive
angular margin between class prototypes and batch-hard triplet
mining~\cite{hermans2017} shaping local distances. K-reciprocal
re-ranking~\cite{zhong2017} is a standard unsupervised post-processing step.

\section{Methodology}
% -------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Dataset and Protocol}

SeaTurtleIDHeads~\cite{wildlifedatasets2023} contains 7,582 images of 400
individual sea turtles collected over more than a decade. All images are cropped
to the head region and vary widely in pose, lighting, camera angle, distance, and
resolution, reflecting real field conditions. Head detection and segmentation are
outside the scope of this work; inputs are assumed pre-cropped.

The dataset is split into training (70\%), validation (20\%), and test (10\%)
partitions by an open-set identity split: no individual appears in more than one
partition, so every reported result concerns identities never seen during
training. Identities with fewer than $N_G + 2$ images are excluded, guaranteeing
$N_G$ gallery images and at least two query images per identity. Within each
evaluation partition, $N_G = 3$ images per identity are randomly assigned to the
gallery and mean-pooled into a single $\ell_2$-normalised prototype; the remainder
form the query set. Validation is used only for checkpoint selection; the test
partition is used once.

\subsection{Preprocessing and Augmentation}

Images are resized to $256 \times 256$. Training augmentations are applied
sequentially: random crop (resize to $288 \times 288$, crop to $256 \times 256$),
random horizontal flip ($p = 0.5$), colour jitter over brightness, contrast,
saturation and hue, random grayscale ($p = 0.1$), random rotation up to
$20^{\circ}$, Gaussian blur (kernel $5 \times 5$, $\sigma \in [0.1, 2.0]$), and
random erasing ($p = 0.3$) to simulate partial occlusion by seagrass or shadow.
At evaluation only resize and normalisation are applied. Pixels are normalised
with ImageNet statistics ($\mu = [0.485, 0.456, 0.406]$,
$\sigma = [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]$).

\subsection{Model Architecture}

\subsubsection{Backbone}
The baseline backbone is ViT-B/16 pretrained with DINOv3~\cite{simeoni2025},
loaded via the PyTorch Image Models (timm) library. A $256 \times 256$ input
yields 256 patch tokens; after 12 Transformer blocks, the 768-dimensional [CLS]
token serves as the global image representation.

\subsubsection{Embedding Head}
The backbone output passes through Layer Normalisation, dropout
($p_{\text{drop}}$), and a bias-free linear projection $768 \to d_e = 256$. The
output is $\ell_2$-normalised, placing all embeddings on the unit hypersphere so
that cosine similarity reduces to a dot product.

\subsection{Loss Functions}

ArcFace~\cite{deng2019} adds an angular margin $m_a$ to the angle between an
embedding and its correct class prototype before softmax cross-entropy:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{ArcFace}} = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
  \log \frac{e^{s_a \cos(\theta_{y_i} + m_a)}}
  {e^{s_a \cos(\theta_{y_i} + m_a)} + \sum_{j \neq y_i} e^{s_a \cos\theta_j}}
  \label{eq:arcface}
\end{equation}

with scale $s_a = 32.0$ and margin $m_a = 0.5$. The scale is set below the value
of 64 used in the original formulation because the identity count here is far
smaller than in large-scale face benchmarks; Section~\ref{sec:sens} confirms this
choice empirically.

Online Hard Triplet Loss~\cite{hermans2017} mines the hardest positive and hardest
negative within each batch:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{Triplet}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
  \max\!\bigl(d(a_i, p_i) - d(a_i, n_i) + m_t,\; 0\bigr)
  \label{eq:triplet}
\end{equation}

where $d(\cdot,\cdot)$ is the Euclidean distance and $m_t = 0.3$. The total
objective is

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{ArcFace}} +
  \lambda_t \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{Triplet}}.
  \label{eq:total}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Training Configuration}

Each batch contains $P = 16$ identities with $K = 4$ images each (batch size 64),
sampled with replacement so that valid positive pairs always exist.
AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019} is used with weight decay $\lambda_w = 10^{-4}$.
Layer-wise Learning Rate Decay (LLRD)~\cite{sun2019} assigns each Transformer
block $i$ of $L = 12$ its own rate:

\begin{equation}
  \eta_i = \eta_{\text{bb}} \cdot \gamma^{L - i}, \quad \gamma = 0.75
  \label{eq:llrd}
\end{equation}

with backbone base rate $\eta_{\text{bb}} = 10^{-5}$, while the randomly
initialised embedding head and ArcFace classifier use
$\eta_{\text{base}} = 10^{-3}$. A linear warmup of $W = 5$ epochs precedes cosine
decay~\cite{loshchilov2017} over $E = 50$ epochs:

\begin{equation}
  \eta(e) = \begin{cases}
    \eta_{\max} \cdot \dfrac{e+1}{W}                                                           & e < W    \\[6pt]
    \dfrac{\eta_{\max}}{2}\!\left(1 + \cos\!\left(\pi \cdot \dfrac{e - W}{E - W}\right)\right) & e \geq W
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:schedule}
\end{equation}

Gradients are clipped at $G_{\max} = 1.0$; validation runs every 5 epochs with
early stopping patience of 10 intervals; automatic mixed precision is enabled.

\subsection{Inference and Evaluation}

Embeddings are extracted with a single forward pass over the resized image and
$\ell_2$-normalised; no test-time augmentation is used, so all reported
differences are attributable to training and to domain, not to inference
protocol. Similarity is cosine, optionally refined by k-reciprocal
re-ranking~\cite{zhong2017}:

\begin{equation}
  D_{\text{final}}(q, g) = \lambda_{rr} D_{\text{cosine}}(q, g)
  + (1 - \lambda_{rr}) D_{\text{Jaccard}}(q, g)
  \label{eq:rerank}
\end{equation}

with $k_1 = 20$, $k_2 = 6$, $\lambda_{rr} = 0.3$. We report Rank-1/5/10 from the
Cumulative Matching Characteristic and mean Average Precision (mAP)~\cite{zheng2015}.

Three groups emerge. \textbf{Sensitive but already optimal:} $\eta_{\text{base}}$,
$s_a$, and image size cannot be improved upon, yet misconfiguring them is costly.
Moving $\eta_{\text{base}}$ by one decade in either direction costs more than
22\,pp of Rank-1, because the randomly initialised head either fails to converge
or never learns within the epoch budget. \textbf{Real headroom:}
$\eta_{\text{bb}}$, $E$, $\lambda_t$, $p_{\text{drop}}$, backbone, $m_a$, and $W$.
Raising the backbone rate from $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-4}$ yields 87.8\% Rank-1 and
91.6\% mAP on its own, showing that the conventional, conservative fine-tuning
rate was the single largest constraint on the system. Since the trend is monotone
and $10^{-4}$ is the upper bound tested, the true optimum may lie higher.
\textbf{Negligible:} the bottom six parameters move Rank-1 by under 2\,pp, which
on a query set of a few hundred amounts to a handful of queries and is not
separable from seed noise.

Two results contradict common practice and deserve emphasis. First, disabling
Triplet Loss entirely ($\lambda_t = 0$) is the best setting, improving Rank-1 to
83.4\%; ArcFace alone is sufficiently discriminative at this dataset scale, and
the triplet term interferes with optimisation. This also removes the
justification for PK-sampling, explaining why $K = 2$ outperforms $K = 4$.
Second, performance degrades monotonically with dropout, and weight decay prefers
its smallest tested value. Together with the absence of overfitting at $E = 50$,
this indicates that the model is not in an overfitting regime at all: the
self-supervised DINOv3 features and the augmentation pipeline already act as
strong implicit regularisation, and explicit regularisation only discards useful
capacity.

\subsection{Combined Configuration and Sub-Additivity}

To test whether parameter effects are additive, a combined configuration adopting
all 12 settings with positive $\Delta$ was trained (Table~\ref{tab:combined}).

  \label{tab:combined}

  \small
  \begin{tabular}{lcccc}

                                      & \multicolumn{2}{c}{\textbf{Cosine}} & \multicolumn{2}{c}{\textbf{+Re-rank}}                                 \\
    \cmidrule(lr){2-3}\cmidrule(lr){4-5}
    \textbf{Configuration}            & R@1                                 & mAP                                   & R@1           & mAP           \\
    \midrule
    Baseline                          & 77.4                                & 83.6                                  & 78.3          & 84.9          \\
    $\eta_{\text{bb}} = 10^{-4}$ only & ---                                 & ---                                   & 87.8          & 91.6          \\
    Combined (12 changes)             & 89.1                                & 92.0                                  & \textbf{90.4} & \textbf{93.5} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The combined configuration gains $+12.1$\,pp Rank-1 and $+8.6$\,pp mAP over the
baseline, a substantial improvement. The comparison against the additive
prediction, however, is the more informative result. Summing the individual
$\Delta$ values gives $+33.7$\,pp, predicting $78.3 + 33.7 = 112.0\%$ Rank-1 --- above
the theoretical maximum. The equivalent mAP calculation predicts 105.6\%. Only
36\% of the predicted Rank-1 gain and 42\% of the predicted mAP gain materialise;
the consistency of these ratios across two metrics indicates a systematic
property rather than an artefact.

The explanation is overlap. Several improvements address the same underlying
limitation and therefore cannot be summed: $\eta_{\text{bb}}$, $E$, $W$, the
larger backbone, and $\gamma$ all give the backbone more capacity or more
opportunity to adapt to the turtle domain, while $\lambda_t = 0$,
$p_{\text{drop}} = 0$, and $\lambda_w = 10^{-5}$ all relax regularisation on a
model that was never overfitting. Consistently, raising $\eta_{\text{bb}}$ alone
already recovers $+9.5$\,pp, i.e. about 78\% of the combined gain; the other
eleven changes contribute $+2.6$\,pp between them.

The methodological consequence is that OFAT usefully ranks parameters by
importance and direction but cannot predict combined performance, and stacking
all OFAT winners does not guarantee an optimum. One case is visible in our own
selection: $\gamma = 0.65$ entered the combined configuration on Rank-1
($+1.2$\,pp) despite reducing mAP and Rank-5 in its own ablation.

\subsection{Open-Set Identification with Rejection}

Field images may show unregistered animals. Adding five identities absent from
the gallery brings the query set to 266 (217 registered, 49 unregistered). We
compare two structurally different rejection rules. The \emph{global threshold}
is absolute: the top-1 cosine score is compared against a single tuned value
(maximising Youden's $J$). The \emph{background class} is relative: prototypes of
turtles outside the gallery are retained as multiple vectors, and a query is
accepted when $s = s_{\text{reg}} - s_{\text{bg}} \geq \text{margin}$, where
$s_{\text{bg}}$ is the maximum similarity to any background prototype. Because
$s_{\text{bg}}$ is recomputed per query, the rule normalises hub identities.
Background prototypes come from a disjoint image source, so both schemes are
evaluated on the identical 266-query set.

unchanged between Table~\ref{tab:cross} and Table~\ref{tab:reject}; only a
rejection stage placed before ranking has been added, and it discards over 40\% of
valid registered queries.

The decisive observation is that both rules land on nearly the same operating
point --- every difference is on the order of one or two queries --- despite being
mechanically unrelated. The cause is visible in the score distributions. All
top-1 scores, registered and unregistered alike, are confined to a narrow band of
roughly $0.85$--$0.98$: because every image shares near-identical structure (a
turtle head against an underwater background), embeddings occupy a narrow cone of
the hypersphere and any two images are already similar before identity is
considered. The unregistered distribution is not merely shifted but embedded
inside the lower tail of the registered distribution, so any threshold high enough
to exclude all unregistered queries necessarily removes a large share of
registered ones. Separability is only moderate, with ROC-AUC of $0.77$--$0.79$,
which bounds every possible decision rule. For the relative scheme, the two
classes overlap almost completely along the $s_{\text{bg}}$ axis, so
$s_{\text{bg}}$ carries no discriminative information and
$s = s_{\text{reg}} - s_{\text{bg}}$ is effectively $s_{\text{reg}}$ plus noise.

Qualitative inspection confirms that the representation is not at fault. Among the
five most confident errors, all are registered queries wrongly rejected --- none is a
false accept --- and in every case the correct identity remains within the top five,
at rank 1 for three of them (scores $0.853$, $0.867$, $0.868$). Retrieval had
already placed the right answer first; the rejection stage discarded it. Among
accepted registered queries, retrieval accuracy is $0.567/(1-0.419) \approx 97.6\%$
for the background class and $\approx 97.7\%$ for the threshold, i.e. both rules are
simply strict enough to filter the hard cases out in advance.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:disc}
% -------------------------------------------------------

Our results separate two failure sources that are usually conflated. The
embedding generalises: it survives a change of dataset with a measured and
moderate loss, and it keeps the correct identity at rank 1 even in the cases the
system reports most confidently and wrongly. What fails is the mapping from
similarity scores to a registered/unregistered decision. This suggests that
future work on wildlife Re-ID deployment should target score calibration and
domain-specific score normalisation rather than further retrieval accuracy: an
extra point of Rank-1 is worth little when 42\% of valid queries are rejected
before ranking.

Several limitations bound these conclusions. All results come from a single seed,
so parameters whose Rank-1 range is below 2\,pp cannot be distinguished from
random fluctuation; multi-seed means and standard deviations are required. The
OFAT baseline sits in an under-trained regime ($E = 100$ still improves over
$E = 50$), so other parameters were assessed at a non-converged operating point.
The rejection threshold was tuned on the same set it was evaluated on and only 49
unregistered queries were available, so the reported 100\% unregistered recall
should not be read as a guarantee on new data. Finally, head detection and
segmentation are assumed rather than integrated.

% -------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
% -------------------------------------------------------

We presented a sea turtle Re-ID system built on a DINOv3-pretrained Vision
Transformer, trained under an open-set identity split on SeaTurtleIDHeads. A
convention-derived baseline reaches 78.3\% Rank-1 and 84.9\% mAP, and a tuned
configuration reaches 90.4\% and 93.5\%. A sensitivity study over 19 parameters
shows that the backbone learning rate dominates, contributing roughly 78\% of the
total gain, that ArcFace alone outperforms the conventional ArcFace-plus-triplet
combination at this data scale, and that OFAT gains are strongly sub-additive and
must not be summed. The model transfers to SeaTurtleID2022 without retraining at
81.1\% Rank-1. Under rejection of unregistered individuals, however, Registered
Rank-1 falls to 55.3--56.7\% at a false-reject rate near 42\%, and two
structurally different decision rules converge on the same operating point because
top-1 scores of registered and unregistered queries are only moderately separable
(ROC-AUC $0.77$--$0.79$). The bottleneck for field deployment is score
calibration, not representation quality. Future work should pursue multi-seed
evaluation, threshold tuning on a held-out validation set with a larger
unregistered population, exploitation of left-right profile
similarity~\cite{papafitsoros2025}, and integration of head detection into an
end-to-end pipeline.
